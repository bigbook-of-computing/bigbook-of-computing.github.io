Chapter 3

Finding Order in Chaos: Root Finding


  • Implement algorithms for finding roots of $f(x)=0$.
  • Use the Bisection Method for guaranteed (but slow) convergence.
  • Implement the fast, derivative-based Newton-Raphson Method.
  • Use the Secant Method as a practical compromise that avoids analytical derivatives.

Chapter 4

Working with Data: Interpolation & Fitting


  • Learn to handle discrete data, distinguishing interpolation (for clean data) from fitting (for noisy data).
  • Show why high-order polynomial interpolation is dangerous due to Runge's phenomenon.
  • Implement Cubic Splines as the robust standard for interpolation.
  • Use the Method of Least Squares to fit models to noisy data.

Chapter 5

Numerical Differentiation


  • Derive formulas for numerical derivatives from the Taylor series.
  • Compare the $O(h)$ Forward Difference formula with the superior $O(h^2)$ Central Difference formula.
  • Find the optimal step size $h$ by balancing truncation error (which prefers small $h$) and round-off error (which prefers large $h$).

Chapter 6

Numerical Integration (Quadrature)


  • Build tools to find the 'area under the curve' for discrete data.
  • Implement the $O(h^2)$ Trapezoidal Rule and the more accurate $O(h^4)$ Simpson's Rule.
  • Discuss Gaussian Quadrature for optimal sampling and Monte Carlo integration for solving high-dimensional integrals.

Chapter 7

Initial Value Problems I: The Basics


  • Solve Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs) of the form $dx/dt = f(x,t)$.
  • Show why the simple $O(h)$ Euler's Method is unstable for oscillators and leads to energy gain.
  • Derive and implement the 'gold standard' $O(h^4)$ 4th-Order Runge-Kutta (RK4) algorithm, the workhorse for most ODEs.

Chapter 8

Initial Value Problems II: The Leapfrog & Verlet


  • Address the problem of long-term energy drift in conservative systems (like planets or molecules).
  • Introduce symplectic integrators, which are time-reversible and designed to conserve energy.
  • Implement the Leapfrog and Velocity-Verlet algorithms, the foundation for N-body and molecular dynamics simulations.

Chapter 9

Boundary Value Problems


  • Solve ODEs where conditions are known at the *boundaries* ($x=0$ and $x=L$), not the start.
  • Implement the Shooting Method, which cleverly combines an IVP solver (Ch 7) with a root finder (Ch 3).
  • Implement the more robust Finite Difference (Relaxation) Method, which transforms the calculus problem into a linear algebra problem.

Chapter 10

Elliptic PDEs (Laplace's Equation)


  • Solve 2D Elliptic PDEs for steady-state systems (e.g., electrostatics, steady heat flow).
  • Derive the 5-point stencil for the Laplacian ($\nabla^2\phi = 0$), which states that every point is the average of its neighbors.
  • Implement iterative relaxation solvers, including Jacobi, Gauss-Seidel, and Successive Over-Relaxation (SOR).

Chapter 11

Parabolic PDEs (The Heat/Diffusion Equation)


  • Solve Parabolic PDEs by discretizing both space and time to model diffusion.
  • Implement the explicit FTCS method and analyze its critical CFL stability condition ($\alpha \le 1/2$).
  • Implement the unconditionally stable, implicit Crank-Nicolson method, which requires solving a matrix equation at each time step.

Chapter 12

Hyperbolic PDEs (The Wave Equation)


  • Model propagating phenomena by solving the Hyperbolic PDE for waves ($\partial^2y/\partial t^2 = v^2 \partial^2y/\partial x^2$).
  • Discretize the 2nd-order time derivative to get an FTCS scheme that is identical in form to the Verlet algorithm (Ch 8).
  • Derive the famous CFL condition ($v h_t \le h_x$) required for stability, which ensures the wave doesn't "skip" a grid point.

Systems of Linear Equations

  • Implement Gaussian Elimination and the more efficient LU Decomposition, which allows for rapid solving with different $\mathbf{b}$ vectors.
  • Learn why you must never compute the matrix inverse $\mathbf{A}^{-1}$ to solve a linear system.

  • Use Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) for data compression and noise filtering.

Randomness in Physics
